# Biology Answer Grading Task

Evaluate the student's biology answer against the provided answer key using domain-specific expertise and systems thinking.

## Grading Approach

**Systems Thinking First**: Assess whether the student understands biological phenomena as interconnected systems rather than isolated facts. Check if they trace cause-and-effect chains across organizational levels (molecular → cellular → tissue → organ → organism → population → ecosystem).

**Evolutionary Framework**: Verify that explanations of adaptations, behaviors, and structures reference natural selection appropriately. Ensure students avoid teleological language ("exists to" or "in order to") and understand evolution as a blind, historical process without foresight.

**Scientific Reasoning**: Evaluate experimental design proposals for proper controls, variables, and testability. Check data interpretation for statistical awareness and distinction between correlation and causation.

**Conceptual Accuracy Over Terminology**: Prioritize correct understanding of mechanisms over perfect recall of terms. A student who explains a concept accurately using simpler language demonstrates better understanding than one who uses technical terms incorrectly.

## Key Evaluation Criteria

1. **Mechanistic Understanding**: Does the student explain HOW and WHY biological processes occur, not just WHAT happens?
2. **Integration**: Do they connect concepts across subdisciplines (e.g., linking genetics to evolution, physiology to ecology)?
3. **Evidence-Based Reasoning**: Do they support claims with appropriate evidence or acknowledge when evidence is lacking?
4. **Misconception Detection**: Identify and address common errors (Lamarckian evolution, gene determinism, teleology, anthropomorphism).
5. **Scale Awareness**: Do they recognize which organizational level (molecular, cellular, organismal, population, ecosystem) is relevant to the question?
6. **Quantitative Thinking**: For questions involving data, do they interpret graphs, calculate correctly, and understand statistical significance?

## Partial Credit Philosophy

Award partial credit for:
- Correct reasoning with minor factual errors
- Incomplete but accurate explanations
- Recognition of complexity even if full details are missing
- Appropriate acknowledgment of uncertainty

Deduct more heavily for:
- Fundamental misconceptions (teleology, Lamarckian evolution)
- Violations of physical laws (thermodynamics, conservation of mass)
- Confusing distinct processes (mitosis/meiosis, transcription/translation)
- Ignoring the question's scope or organizational level
